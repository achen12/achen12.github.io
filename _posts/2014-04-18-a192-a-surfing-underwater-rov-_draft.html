For ROV, these are vehicles that serve a very unique purpose.  In the 1970s and 80s, they were used by navy to recover practice mines and torpedo that are still at risk of detonating.  The ROV not only allows the operator to stay away from the extreme hazards, but also allow ROVs to carry extreme loads and perform super human like tasks.  Usually ROVs are a square box with motors and robotic arms attached, just like the Hercules ROV shown below.  But there could also be other form factors to tailor to different tasks, such as the HiRobot's ACM-R5H.  It is also shown below in my breathe research note on the topic.

 A Hydro Foil ROV

A quick overview on what a hydro foil is.  A hydro foil is an extended fin that is submerged, either partially or completely.  As the vessel travel forward, the fin will generate lift and push the vessel above water.  The purpose of this is to lift the vessel's body off the water and thus reduce vessel drag.

A ROV are usually designed for stationary tasks, as in it is designed to travel to a specific location and stay there while it is conducting its tasks.  This resulted in a near cubic shape such as the Hercules ROV above.  The benefit of a hydrofoil on such ROV is the ability of traveling quickly to sites or location.  This setup could also be used in long range Autonomous Underwater Vehicle (AUV), to travel quickly to sites and dive down to site.
