In the last episode....  Team 3 has to solve The Grand Challenge.  Their robot will land on Mars (Model Mars... i.e. a Maze) with cubes (living quarter modules) landed all around the maze.  Their objective is to have the robot collect the modules and build a small structure with the cubes before the astronauts landed (in 10 minutes).  Now.... How could they make the RSS robot in 4 weeks to do that?....

We.... Didn't...  Or other words,  we build a whole another robot to solve the problem.  Now, why is that?  This is mainly because of the short 4 week period.  This is a critical point.  Most of the members including me are robot competition veterans.  We understand that with only 4 weeks and with only a single run for the final competition, we need to think low tech.  Now, in a typical robot design, we can typically split the robot into two levels: high and low.  Low level portion of the robot include the basic mechanical design, sensors, and simple control loops.  High level includes sensory analytics(Mapping) and decision making (Path Planning).  To increase the robustness of the robot, we focused down on the low level designs.  And this is what we came up with...

Unlike the RSS bot, the new robot will not use an arm to pick up the cubes with its arm, but just roll the cube up with a ramp and funnel it to a wall hopper on the back.  You might be thinking... This is a very poor CAD.... And yes, it is.  Here's the actual CAD.

[Insert CAD pic]

So as you can see, we have solidified a lot of things.  There's a kinect camera mounted inverted on the top of the robot.  We have chosen to use the kinect, because we can mark identified cubes very easily and reliably.  Also the robot increased in height to house more layers of cubes in the back hopper.  So what does it actually look like when built?

 Front
Side
As you can see, there are a lot of changes between the CAD and the actual robot.  First the sonar wall sensors are placed higher to avoid detecting the cubes by mistake.  There are two computers on board.  First the Mac tucked away in the lower deck to process the Kinect data and scout for the road ahead.  It'll then report to the netbook upstairs via ROS (Robot Operating System).  The netbook then talk with the microcontrollers to actuate the whole robot.  Thirdly, there is an added rubber band in the middle to be used as a front bump sensor.  It will trigger a toggle switch placed on the left and tell the brain that it hit something in front.  In the 4 weeks, we focused down on every low level designs we can use to avoid using any of the complex high level items.

In the software side, we made some overhaul from the RSS robot, too.  In hope of increasing our connectivity speed, we migrated our code base from ROS Electric to the newest (at the time) ROS Hydro.  We essentially abandoned our code in the RSS bot and re-programed everything ourselves.  Thankfully, because of the modularity that the ROS system provide us, we can easily divide up the tasks by specifying the inter module messages before hand.  We were doing it so well in fact.  In the 48 hour time window between our first ungraded trial run and the actual run, we abandoned all of our code again and restarted.  Because of the modular frame work, the five of us can code relatively fast on a small code base (~10^3 lines).
